12 Sources of Startup Capital You MUST Know About - Part [x] of Fundraising Guide



There is no one-fit-all funding source when it comes to startups. Every startup is unique based on its industry, needs, challenges, potential, size & scale - So, are the funding sources.

No matter how good the product or service is, a startup needs funds to launch & scale. While many founders invest their savings only sometimes is it adequate to meet their goals.

This is when startups get on a spree to raise capital via different sources. 

When it comes to funding your business, several types of financing options are available, depending on the stage of your company—whether you’re in the startup phase, at mid-level growth, or running a high-level, mature enterprise.

Keep reading to learn about the different sources of capital and why it may be the best option for you.

.

1. Startup Stage Funding

This is often the most challenging stage to secure funding, as startups are typically unproven with little to no revenue. However, several options are available:

a. Bootstrapping

  • It is all about typically using your own savings or revenue from early sales to fund the business.

  • Bootstrapping is the way to go in the early days when outside funding is either not an option or not desirable.

  • According to a 2023 report by Fundera, 77% of small businesses rely on personal savings to get started. While this can give you full control, it also comes with the risk of draining personal finances.


b. Friends and Family

Yes, it's borrowing money from people you know. All big businesses you know today would have gone through this as it’s best when you need small amounts of capital and prefer informal agreements.

Slack, the popular communication tool, started with $17,000 from family and friends.


c. Angel Investors

High-net-worth individuals who invest their funds in exchange for equity are called angel investors.

Typically in the early seed stage, when the business is still in development or pre-revenue approach angel investors

In 2022, angel investors in the U.S. provided $25.3 billion in funding, according to the Angel Capital Association. Well-known companies like Google and WhatsApp started with angel investment.

In recent days, angels have been increasingly interested in sectors like AI, biotech, and sustainable energy. 


d. Crowdfunding

Raising small amounts of money from a large number of people, usually via online platforms (Kickstarter, Indiegogo) is known as crowdfunding.

Though not-so-specific, the Ideal for product-based businesses with strong consumer appeal.

In 2023, the global crowdfunding market was valued at $13.9 billion, and it’s expected to grow at a rate of 16% annually. Oculus VR raised $2.4 million via Kickstarter before being acquired by Facebook.

Equity crowdfunding is becoming more popular, allowing contributors to become shareholders.


e. Seed Funding

Seed funding is nothing but early-stage capital, often provided by venture capital firms in exchange for equity.

This type of funding works when you want to develop a product or prove your business model.

Did you know? Robinhood raised $3 million in seed funding from Index Ventures in its early days.





2. Mid-Level Growth Funding

At this stage, the company is scaling, and more substantial capital is needed to grow operations, expand into new markets, or develop new products.

a. Venture Capital (VC)

VC Funding is funding from professional investment firms that typically invest in high-growth potential startups in exchange for equity.

Businesses go for this type when scaling up, entering new markets, or developing major product innovations. For instance, Airbnb raised $112 million in a Series C round, helping them to expand globally.

While fintech and SaaS remain popular, investors have been cautious in 2024, focusing more on profitability than rapid growth.

VC funding hit $445 billion globally in 2021, though it cooled off slightly in 2023, with total funding dropping to $270 billion due to economic uncertainty.


b. Private Equity

Private equity (PE) firms invest large sums of money in exchange for ownership or control of the company. Ideal for businesses that have already proven their business model and need capital to expand aggressively or restructure prefer PE.

Global private equity investments amounted to $1.2 trillion in 2023, according to Bain & Company. 

A popular example of private equity is the PE firm Blackstone acquired Ancestry.com for $4.7 billion, helping the company expand its DNA services globally.


c. Mezzanine Financing

Mezzanine Financing is the hybrid form of debt and equity financing that allows lenders to convert to equity if the loan isn’t paid back on time.

If your company has strong growth potential but you don’t want to give up too much equity this is one of the best options.

Spotify used mezzanine financing before its IPO to fund expansion without diluting ownership.


d. Revenue-Based Financing

In revenue-based financing, Investors provide capital in exchange for a percentage of monthly revenue until the principal and interest are paid back.

Great for businesses with consistent revenue but no tangible assets to secure traditional loans opt for revenue-based financing

Popular in e-commerce and SaaS industries, revenue-based financing has become a $5 billion market globally.



3. High-Level / Mature Company Funding

At this stage, companies often require capital for acquisitions, international expansion, or going public.

a. Initial Public Offering (IPO)

IPO is a very popular process of offering shares of a private corporation to the public in a new stock issuance.

Companies go for IPO when they have strong financials and want to raise significant capital to fuel expansion or pay down debt.

There were 181 IPOs in 2023 in the U.S., raising a total of $21 billion, down from the highs of the previous few years.

Rivian Automotive raised $11.9 billion in 2021, marking one of the largest IPOs in recent years.

The IPO market has been sluggish in 2024 due to macroeconomic conditions, but companies like Arm (semiconductor) are expected to go public soon.


b. Corporate Bonds

Large companies issue debt securities to investors, promising to pay back the loan with interest through corporate bonds.

When your company has a strong credit rating and you don’t want to dilute equity. If you are not aware, Apple routinely issues bonds to raise billions, despite having significant cash reserves, because the cost of borrowing is low.


c. Merger & Acquisition (M&A) Financing

As the name suggests, this type of funding is raised to acquire other businesses or merge with them.

Big businesses use this as a strategy to grow rapidly by absorbing competitors or entering new markets. The M&A market remains robust in the tech and healthcare sectors.

In 2023, Microsoft completed its $69 billion acquisition of Activision Blizzard, showcasing how M&A can be a strategic growth tool.


So, what about the A,B,C,D on funding we often encounter? Yes, we hear you asking that.


Series Funding

Series funding is the traditional type of fundraising that can be quite a combination of different capital sources. 

As per its name, the startups advance through a series of pre-set stages mentioned below in series funding.

Series Funding

  • Pre-seed funding 

  • Seed funding

  • Series A funding

  • Series B funding

  • Series C funding

  • Series D+ funding

Pre-seed Funding

Pre-seed funding is typically the first round of investment for startups, raising around $600,000. At this stage, founders often rely on personal savings, friends, and family to fund early development. This phase covers initial expenses like product development and setting up operations. It’s also referred to as "bootstrapping."

Seed Funding

Seed funding, usually around $2.9 million, helps startups with market research and product refinement. This phase is crucial for turning ideas into viable products. Investors are often angel investors or venture capitalists.

Series A Funding

In Series A, businesses that have achieved product-market fit raise around $11.6 million. This funding focuses on scaling the business, growing the customer base, and optimizing the product for a larger market.

Series B Funding

Series B funding, averaging $30 million, is for businesses that need capital to meet growing demand. This stage helps fund marketing, hiring, and technology development as the company scales more aggressively.

Series C Funding

Series C, with an average of $60 million, helps companies expand horizontally. This could involve acquisitions, launching new products, or entering new markets. Investors at this stage expect a significant return as the business is more established.

Series D+ Funding

Series D or later rounds, raising around $105 million, are less common. They usually happen when businesses need extra capital to continue growing or prepare for an IPO. These rounds may also occur if previous funding didn’t deliver the expected results.


Key Trends & Takeaways

  • AI and Green Tech Boom: Startups in artificial intelligence, green technology, and biotechnology are attracting record investments. According to PitchBook, AI startups alone raised over $25 billion in 2023.

  • Profitability Focus: Investors in 2024 are more focused on profitability and sustainable growth, as opposed to the high-risk, high-reward strategies of the 2010s.

  • Alternative Financing: With tighter VC markets, businesses are increasingly turning to alternative funding sources like crowdfunding, revenue-based financing, and PE funds.

Understanding the right type of funding for your business at each stage is critical. The landscape continues to evolve, with new trends and shifts in investor focus, so staying adaptable is key.
